Research Summary

As a researcher and engineer, my work focuses on the invention, development, and application of soft robotics technologies to address critical challenges in mobility assistance and neurorehabilitation. With a strong foundation in experimental methodologies, I prioritize solutions that are both practical and innovative. My research integrates principles from mechanical engineering and biomedical sciences, aiming to enhance human mobility and improve patient outcomes through cutting-edge technological advancements.

Research Interests

Assistive Robotics, Soft Robotics, Mechatronics Design, Modeling and Control, Human-robot Interaction, Machine Learning, Reinforcement Learning, Artificial Intelligence (AI)


1. Experiment-free Exoskeleton Assistance via Learning in Simulation

Can wearable robots be intelligent, versatile, and easy to use to improve human health and quality of life? We presented a “learning-in-simulation” framework to rapidly develop robot controllers to assist multi-locomotion activities without relying on any human-involved experiments - one of the grand challenges of wearable robots that have significantly hindered their widespread adoption. Our reinforcement learning-based method does not require any human tests or handcrafting of control rules. The learned controller can be directly deployed on a portable exoskeleton which immediately reduces energy consumption during walking, running, and stair climbing. Unlike previous achievements of reinforcement learning that primarily focused on simulation or board games (e.g., AlphaGo) or robots only, we proposed a new method, data-driven and physics-informed deep reinforcement learning, to control wearable robots to directly benefit humans.

2. Stiffness Model-Based Continuous Torque Control

Can exoskeletons generate a large range of stiffness for versatile locomotion assistance? Can we control exoskeletons continuously instead of in discrete states? Exoskeletons using series elastic actuation have a small range of stiffness, thus unsuitable for versatile locomotion assistance. Exoskeleton controllers typically rely on discrete control (e.g., finite state machines) causing jerky assistive torque to human wearers. To fill this gap, we electrify robot actuation via high torque density motors and small ratio gears (quasi-direct drive (QDD) actuation). We created a lightweight (1.6 kg unilateral mass) portable knee exoskeleton and developed a stiffness-based continuous controller. QDD actuation enables electrical adjusting of the exoskeleton stiffness across a wide range (0-350 Nm/rad > human knee joint stiffness 0-176 Nm/rad). Our controller can estimate joint moments accurately using only two portable IMU sensors so that the exoskeleton can continuously synchronize with human motion and provide synergistic control. Thus, the robot generates a continuous torque profile and adapts to changes in varying gait patterns.

3. Collocated Torque Control of Portable Exoskeleton Without Torque Sensors

Torque-controlled exoskeletons require stable controller gains to ensure consistent performance across various activities. However, current exoskeleton torque controllers are limited by a narrow range of stable gains and often rely on expensive sensors or produce large errors in torque estimation. To address these issues, I developed a unified method to quantify the stability margin for exoskeletons with series elastic actuators (SEA) or quasi-direct drive (QDD) actuators and introduced a collocated impedance controller that is intrinsically stable with a highly accurate torque estimator. Our controller achieves significantly higher stiffness (900 Nm/rad at 2 Hz vs. 400 Nm/rad in current systems) and reduces torque estimation errors to 3%-5%, compared to the 10%-21% errors of current estimators. This work improves exoskeleton control stability and accuracy without costly sensors, advancing the development of portable and affordable exoskeletons for broader community use.

4. Actuator Optimization and Control of Pediatric Knee Exoskeleton

Our goal is to enable a paradigm shift in pediatric exoskeletons, moving them from the clinic to everyday community settings. To achieve this, we developed new hardware and software solutions. On the hardware side, we optimized the actuator to be lightweight and adjustable to accommodate children's growth. For the software, we created a deep learning-based algorithm that ensures robust gait phase estimation, even for children with cerebral palsy who have irregular gait patterns. These innovations work together to make pediatric exoskeletons more practical and effective in real-world environments, helping children move more freely.

5. Spatial Iterative Learning Torque Control of Robotic Exoskeletons

High-performance torque tracking is crucial for accurate control of the magnitude and timing of exoskeleton assistive torque profiles. However, state-of-the art torque control methods, e.g., iterative learning control (ILC), applied to exoskeletons cannot achieve satisfying accuracy and convergence speed. We designed a spatial iterative learning (sIL)-based torque control strategy for exoskeletons to achieve accurate and fast torque assistance.

6. Built A Real-time Control Platform

I developed a real-time control platform for semi-portable applications, focusing on the integration of control algorithms and biomechanics science. While the electronics components are stationary, the mechatronic system itself is designed to be portable, enabling flexible experimentation and deployment in various environments. This platform bridges the gap between advanced control theory and practical biomechanics research, facilitating in-depth understanding and testing of these systems in real-time.
